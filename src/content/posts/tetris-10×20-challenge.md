Right out the gate, my initial thoughts were to, instead of trying to brute force an answer for a 10×20 grid, try splitting the grid into 2 halves (either a 10×10 or 20×5 grid) and solve one half, and the other half would just be a reflection of the solution of the first half. I'd also reckoned that I would be limited to only 25 pieces per half. But, here came my first misstep. Looking at the amount of each piece given (which was 7, for most pieces) it was clear that there was not going to be an equal distribution of some of the pieces. Still, I thought that each grid would get 3.5 pieces, and since every piece was of length 4, the grid would use only 2 squares of the piece and the other 2 would spill over into the other grid. In other words, the line of symmetry (or rather the squares surrounding it) between the 2 grids would be filled with the pieces whose amount is 7, acting as a bridge between the 2 grids. At this point, I began contemplating whether this puzzle even had a mathematical way of solving it. I thought that one just had to play around with configurations until they found a solution. But, then I had an idea: since 6 of the 7 piece types were allotted 7 copies each and one (the T piece) had 8 pieces, we could potentially isolate a 196-square sub problem (using 49 pieces) and leave out a single 4‑square piece which would be filled with that extra T-piece. This idea arose from trying to figure out why one set of pieces got one more piece than the others. 

Trying to build on this idea, I was focused on deducing where that extra T piece would go, so I could start constructing the rest of the pieces around that slot. I determined that it couldn’t be placed vertically at the edge of the grid, and was in the process of concluding that it also couldn’t be placed in the middle because there was an even number of rows, which meant that there wasn’t going to be a perfect middle that allowed a repeatable construct implementable on both sides of the grid (i.e. there wasn’t a way to cover both sides and leave a spot in the middle that allowed that extra piece to slide into it and connect the two halves and complete the grid).

As I was contemplating some more, I realised I made a crucial error. When I was contemplating a symmetric solution (splitting the grid into halves, solving one half, which would automatically solve the other half and thus the fill grid) I was thinking that I would split each half, cover the squares in the grids in such a way that for each half, there’d be 2 squares around the line of symmetry that would be left uncovered and would be filled by the extra piece. However, my error lay in a calculation mix-up. I had thought that by removing that one extra piece, I would be left with 50 shapes, which would allow for equal distribution to both halves. However, with there being only 50 pieces, removing that one extra piece, I’d actually be left with 49 pieces, which would mean a 25/24 split among the 2 halves. This realization actually revived the symmetry idea. Here, I saw that you could now evenly divide the 50 pieces across 2 halves, which would mean that I could potentially solve one grid, implicitly solving the other and find the solution.

## Reflection
Wow! Just…wow! This puzzle was, to me, the first real opportunity to do what I set out to do when I embarked on this journey. Don’t get me wrong, the other puzzles were challenging in their own respect, but they were still trivial to solve. This puzzle actually required persistence, resilience, “comfort with the uncomfortable”, as well as an almost delusional level of determination. Additionally, despite struggling and wrestling with this problem over many hours and days (and at this point, I think weeks 😬😂), I believe it was a worthwhile episode, as I experienced a growth in mathematical skill. As I said in my first post, most math questions we encounter during our studies are, unfortunately, simply rote drills – just algorithmically and repeatedly executing some method we learnt a short while ago. However, mathematical research involves lateral thinking, creativity, patience, cognitive flexibility etc. This felt like a mini mathematical research project. From the many, many dead-ends and subsequent forced pivots, to the late night insights to the feelings of despondence, to the many, many hours of contemplation to the euphoric dopamine release when the solution came (I know I'm making this sound like I solved the breakthrough of the century and didn't just fill a grid 😂 but still), I believe this experience was beneficial in honing my mathematical abilities such as proof-forming, corollary extraction,and logical deductions and I’m now even more convinced that this series was a good decision.
